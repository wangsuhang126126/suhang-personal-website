# Why I Started Building My Personal Website Seriously

For a long time, I did not feel a particularly strong need to have a personal website.

I had LinkedIn. I also had a WeChat public account in the past. From time to time, I wanted to write something, but most of the content ended up scattered across different platforms. Some of it looked like a résumé. Some felt closer to social updates. Some was only temporary notes.

Those platforms could show parts of who I am, but they were not ideal places for long-term accumulation.

Recently, I started to rethink this.

If someone works across markets, languages, and industries, and also wants to keep learning about energy, technology, AI, and the Japanese market, relying only on social platforms is not enough. Social platforms are useful for distribution, but not necessarily for accumulation. They are more like public squares than personal studies.

I wanted a space of my own.

## A personal website is not just an online résumé

When I first started building this website, I also thought of it as a better-looking online résumé.

But as the site took shape, I realized it should be more than that.

A résumé is designed to help someone quickly decide whether you are suitable for a role. It has to compress, filter, and emphasize outcomes. It focuses on what you have done.

A personal website can hold something broader.

It can record:

* what questions I am paying attention to;
* how I understand an industry;
* how I use AI to support research and expression;
* what I observe while living and working in Japan;
* what small projects and experiments I build;
* how my career path gradually forms a clearer thread.

These things may not all belong on a résumé, but they say a lot about how a person thinks.

## Why now

One direct reason is AI.

In the past, maintaining a personal website required a lot of effort. Writing articles, organizing research, designing pages, developing the site, and deploying updates all required different skills. Any one of those steps could become a reason to stop.

Now the situation is different.

AI cannot make judgments for me. It cannot replace my point of view or take responsibility for what I publish. But it can make many slow processes faster: organizing material, drafting, translating, checking logic, supporting development, and improving layout.

For the first time, I feel that a personal website is no longer only for programmers or designers. It can become long-term infrastructure for any professional who wants to think, write, and build in public.

More importantly, AI has made me realize that the key question is not whether we can generate content. The real question is whether we can keep asking better questions, form our own judgments, and express them clearly.

A personal website can support that process.

## What I want this website to hold

At this stage, I do not see this website as a single-topic blog. I see it as a long-term personal platform.

It has several parts.

The first is Writing. This is where I will publish essays and observations on energy systems, the Japanese market, AI tools, career development, and everyday life.

The second is Journey. This is where I organize my professional path: from international business to solar energy, and now toward energy storage and broader energy systems. The goal is not to package my experience, but to understand how a clearer direction has gradually emerged.

The third is Lab. This is where I will place small tools and experiments. These may include a battery storage simulator, AI-assisted writing workflows, small web projects, or even personal projects with a more playful side.

The fourth is About and Contact. These sections make the site more than a container for content. They help readers understand the person behind the work and when it makes sense to get in touch.

## Language is part of the site

I plan to make this website gradually available in Chinese, Japanese, and English.

This is not just to make the site look international. It reflects the reality of my life and work.

Chinese is my native language and the language in which I think most naturally. Japanese is the language I use every day while working and living in Japan. English connects me to a wider world of information, technology, and international business discussions.

Writing the same article in three languages is not just translation. It forces me to check my own expression. Is the idea clear in Chinese? Does it sound natural in Japanese? Is it direct enough in English?

That process is itself a form of training.

## I do not want this to become a one-time project

Many personal websites begin with enthusiasm and then stop being updated.

I want to avoid that.

That is why I do not want this website to be only a design project. I want it to become a system that can be maintained over time. Articles can be published continuously. Pages can be improved gradually. Small tools can be added. Content can accumulate.

That is also why I am taking the article system, multilingual publishing flow, GitHub workflow, and Cloudflare deployment process seriously. They may look like technical details, but the goal is simple:

make publishing simple enough so that continuous updating becomes possible.

## What comes next

This website will not be perfect from the beginning.

The design, structure, writing, article system, tool pages, and mobile experience will all continue to change. The site will also evolve as my work and interests evolve.

But I hope it gradually becomes a clear personal space.

Not a place to present a finished identity, but a place to record someone who is still changing, still learning, and still trying to express things more clearly.

That is why I have started building this personal website seriously.
